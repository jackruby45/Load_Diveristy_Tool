Section 1: The Core Principles of Load Diversity

What is Load Diversity?

Load diversity is the fundamental principle that not all gas appliances in a building will be operating at their maximum capacity at the exact same time. The total peak demand of a system is almost always less than the sum of the maximum possible demands of all its individual components.1

Think of it like the lights in a house: while a home may have dozens of light bulbs, it's extremely rare for every single one to be switched on simultaneously. Similarly, a furnace, water heater, and oven are unlikely to all be running at their highest setting at the same moment.

By accounting for this diversity, engineers can accurately size gas meters and piping systems. This prevents costly oversizing while ensuring the system can safely and reliably meet the building's actual peak demand.1

The Diversity Factor Explained

A Diversity Factor is a percentage applied to an appliance's maximum load (its BTU rating) to estimate its realistic contribution to the system's peak demand.1 A factor of 100% implies the appliance is expected to run at full capacity during the system's peak, while a lower factor acknowledges it will likely be off or running at partial load.

The core of the calculation for each appliance is a simple multiplication:

Diversified Load = Max Input Rating × (Diversity Factor / 100)

Section 2: The Sizing Methodology

Applying the principle of diversity involves a clear, two-step methodology: analyzing the potential peak load for each season and then designing for the absolute worst-case scenario.

Seasonal Peak Analysis: The Worst-Case Design Method

Residential natural gas demand is not constant throughout the year; it peaks differently in winter and summer. Sizing a gas service correctly requires identifying the single worst-case demand scenario, which is determined by comparing the calculated Winter Peak load against the Summer Peak load. The larger of the two becomes the final Design Load.

  • Winter Peak Load: In cold climates like MA, NH, and ME, this is typically the highest demand period. It includes the diversified loads of all heating equipment running concurrently with year-round appliances like water heaters and cooking ranges.
  • Summer Peak Load: For homes with high-demand seasonal appliances, the summer load can sometimes exceed the winter load. This calculation includes year-round appliances plus large summer-only loads like pool or spa heaters.

By calculating both scenarios and designing for the higher value, the utility ensures the gas service can safely meet demand on the coldest winter day and on a summer day when the pool heater is running at full capacity.

Winter Peak = Σ(Diversified Winter Loads) + Σ(Diversified Year-Round Loads)
Summer Peak = Σ(Diversified Summer Loads) + Σ(Diversified Year-Round Loads)

Rationale for 100% Diversity Factors (Non-Diversifiable Loads)

It may seem counterintuitive to assign a 100% diversity factor, but it is a critical practice for certain types of equipment. These are considered "non-diversifiable" loads because they must be able to operate at their full rated capacity whenever they are called upon, regardless of other appliance usage. Undersizing the gas system for these loads could lead to equipment failure when it is needed most.2

  • Life-Safety Equipment: An emergency generator is a prime example. It is a life-safety device that must have access to its full required fuel supply during a power outage. Its load can never be discounted.2
  • Critical High-Demand Utilities: A driveway boiler for a snow-melt system is a prime example. Unlike a furnace that cycles, a snow-melt system is a mission-critical utility designed to run at 100% of its rated capacity for many hours continuously during a snow or ice storm. Its function is to prevent ice accumulation and maintain safe access, a task that cannot be compromised. When its sensors detect freezing temperatures and moisture, it demands its full fuel supply without interruption. Therefore, gas utility engineering standards mandate that it be treated as a non-diversifiable load to guarantee the system can perform this critical safety function when needed most.2
  • Initial High-Demand Loads: A pool or spa heater, when initially heating a large volume of cold water, will run continuously at 100% capacity for many hours or even days. The system must be able to support this prolonged peak demand.2

A Practical Example

Let's tie these concepts together. Consider a simple home with three appliances, using the "Utility Standard (Default)" factors:

  • Forced Air Furnace: 100,000 BTU/hr (75% Diversity)
  • Tank Water Heater: 40,000 BTU/hr (35% Diversity)
  • Gas Range/Oven: 65,000 BTU/hr (15% Diversity)

First, we find the Total Connected Load, which is the sum of the maximum ratings:

100,000 + 40,000 + 65,000 = 205,000 BTU/hr

Next, we apply the diversity factors to find the Total Diversified Load:

  • Furnace: 100,000 BTU/hr × 0.75 = 75,000 BTU/hr
  • Water Heater: 40,000 BTU/hr × 0.35 = 14,000 BTU/hr
  • Range: 65,000 BTU/hr × 0.15 = 9,750 BTU/hr
  • Total: 75,000 + 14,000 + 9,750 = 98,750 BTU/hr

The expected peak load (98,750 BTU/hr) is less than half the total possible load (205,000 BTU/hr). The gas service would be sized based on this more realistic diversified load.

Section 3: The Three Sizing Standards

An Overview of the Standards

Diversity factors are not arbitrary. They are derived from engineering studies, historical utility data, and established codes. This calculator includes factors from three recognized sources, each with a different philosophy:

  • Utility Standard (Default): A balanced set of factors based on common practices from North American gas utilities. This provides a good general-purpose estimate for typical residential projects and often represents a middle ground.2
  • ASHRAE (Conservative): Factors derived from the American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers (ASHRAE) handbooks, particularly the ASHRAE Handbook—Fundamentals. These values are often higher (more conservative), leading to a larger calculated diversified load. This approach provides a greater margin of safety.1
  • IAPMO (Lenient): Factors related to the International Association of Plumbing and Mechanical Officials (IAPMO), often found in plumbing and gas codes like the Uniform Mechanical Code (UMC). These values can be lower (more lenient), resulting in a smaller calculated load.3

The following sections provide a detailed analysis of each standard, including its origins, intended use case, and supporting documentation.

The "Utility Standard" Method Explained

Short Answer

The Utility Standard method is a utility‑side diversity approach for premise‑level gas load sizing. You list each residential gas appliance, apply a realistic demand (coincidence) factor to its nameplate input (BTU/h), sum the diversified loads, check winter vs summer peak, convert to CFH, and size the service line, meter, and service regulator to that diversified peak (not the 100% connected load). It is not used to size interior building piping governed by NFPA 54/IFGC.

What It Is (A Precise Definition)

  • A diversity‑based sizing method for utility assets (service pipe, meter, service regulator) at a single residence.
  • Uses per‑appliance demand factors (e.g., 75% for a typical furnace, 35% for a 40‑gal tank water heater, 15% for a range) that reflect the probability of simultaneous operation during the premise’s peak hour.
  • Computes diversified winter and diversified summer totals and designs to the larger of the two.
  • Keeps generators, pool/spa heaters, MAUs, and snow‑melt systems at 100% (no diversity) because they draw near nameplate capacity when they run.
  • The “Utility Standard (Default)” column is a balanced, middle‑of‑the‑road set of factors—stricter than lenient/IAPMO‑style assumptions and looser than conservative/ASHRAE‑style simultaneity—intended for typical New England residential service/meter work.

In utility language, these multipliers are demand factors (also called coincidence factors): “a number, ≤1.0, multiplied by connected load to calculate gas demand; it represents the maximum fraction of the total connected load expected to burn at any time.”

— Con Edison

Where It Comes From (Provenance and Precedent)

  1. Established Utility Practice (Published Specifications)

    Many North American gas utilities explicitly use demand/diversification factors for service sizing and meter/regulator selection. For example, Con Edison (NYC) — Service Sizing specification G‑8200 defines demand factor (“also known as coincidence factor”) and directs designers to apply it. Similarly, CPS Energy (San Antonio) — Gas Service Standards state that “a diversification factor may be applied.” These are clear, modern examples of utility‑side diversity being part of the official design workflow for service and meter sizing.

  2. Codified Recognition of Diversity (State Code Amendment Example)

    While interior gas piping is generally sized to maximum connected demand, some jurisdictions explicitly codify diversity for certain appliances. In Massachusetts, 248 CMR adds §5.4.2.3 Diversity Factor for Domestic Ranges with a demand table for multi‑family buildings (e.g., 2 ranges → 85%, 4 → 60%, … 24+ → 27%). This is a concrete, state‑level acknowledgment of diversity in gas load calculations.

  3. Broader Engineering Lineage (Coincidence/Diversity Concepts)

    The underlying mathematics—coincidence, diversity, and demand factors—are standard in load engineering. Authoritative energy‑engineering references like those from The Department of Energy (Energy.gov) define these terms. Utilities have long adapted these concepts from power/thermal systems to natural gas service sizing.

How It Differs from Building-Code Pipe Sizing (An Important Boundary)

  • NFPA 54/IFGC for Interior Piping: Interior gas piping is normally sized to serve maximum demand at each outlet using the code’s tables or equations. Many code commentaries state the basis as total connected hourly load (all appliances at full rating). That’s an AHJ issue inside the building, separate from the utility’s service/meter design.
  • Utility Standard Method: This is a utility engineering practice for the assets up to and including the meter and service regulator—not a substitute for interior piping calculations.

Why It's Called "Utility Standard (Default)"

It is a curated, middle‑band set of demand factors designed to reflect what many utilities actually assume operationally for residential services—rather than a single national code table. Published utility specs (e.g., Con Edison G‑8200, CPS Energy Standards) demonstrate the method, and other utilities use similar judgment internally. The compiled “Utility Standard” column is therefore a defensible baseline calibrated to typical New England usage and practice, intended to avoid over‑building while preserving a safety margin.

The One-Sentence Explanation

“Utility Standard is our default diversity‑based method for service/meter/regulator sizing: multiply each residential appliance’s nameplate by a realistic demand factor, sum winter and summer peaks, design to the larger, and keep code‑required interior piping sizing separate.”

1) Plain‑English summary

The ASHRAE (Conservative) method applies a higher simultaneity (i.e., higher percentage operating at the same time) to each residential gas appliance than our “Utility Standard” column. Multiply each appliance’s nameplate input by the ASHRAE column factor (e.g., 90% for a furnace <225 kBtuh, 50% for a 40‑gal DHW tank, 20% for a range), sum diversified loads, check winter vs. summer, convert to CFH, and size the service, meter, and service regulator to the larger peak. 2) What “ASHRAE (Conservative)” means here

Conceptual basis. ASHRAE formalizes the ideas of diversity, coincident demand, and coincidence across HVAC and building systems. In ASHRAE’s terminology, diversity factor and coincident demand quantify how much of the connected capacity is likely to operate at the same time as the peak of interest. terminology.ashrae.org

Why conservative? ASHRAE’s load methods aim to capture true peak (coincident) conditions. Adopting this column pushes simultaneity upward, increasing the diversified load (and thus meter/regulator sizing margin). ASHRAE guidance and tools (e.g., the Weather Data Viewer) explicitly analyze mean coincident variables—another reflection of peak‑coincidence philosophy. ASHRAE, unmethours.com

Not a single national table from ASHRAE. ASHRAE does not publish a universal list of residential gas‑appliance simultaneity percentages. Instead, ASHRAE provides the framework (definitions, coincident‑demand approach, end‑use chapters like Service Water Heating with probabilistic/fixture‑based peaking), leaving the percent mapping to the engineer/operator. The “ASHRAE (Conservative)” column in your table is therefore an operator‑standardized mapping aligned with ASHRAE’s coincident‑peak philosophy. ASHRAE, The Department of Energy's Energy.gov

Example of ASHRAE’s diversity in practice (not gas‑specific): An official ASHRAE interpretation for Standard 62 notes that HVAC design procedures incorporate a diversity factor for people and for supply air—showing the organization’s long‑standing use of diversity/coincidence in sizing methodology. ASHRAE

Interior piping is governed by NFPA 54 (National Fuel Gas Code) as adopted/amended locally; code‑table methods generally size piping to meet maximum demand at each outlet. That work is separate from the utility’s service/meter design that uses diversity.

5) How to apply (operator procedure)

  1. Inventory all appliances with nameplate BTU/h.
  2. Pick one column for the job—here, ASHRAE (Conservative) for all appliances.
  3. Multiply nameplate × factor to get per‑appliance diversified BTU/h.
  4. Compute Winter Peak (heating + DHW + cooking + dryer + fireplaces + generator) and Summer Peak (pool/spa + DHW + cooking + outdoor + generator).
  5. Design to the larger of Winter vs. Summer diversified totals.
  6. CFH conversion: CFH ≈ BTU/h ÷ HHV (use system HHV; if unknown, the U.S. average is about 1,036 Btu/ft³). U.S. Energy Information Administration
  7. Meter selection: Select a meter whose rated CFH at ½″ w.c. ΔP ≥ diversified CFH (e.g., AC‑250 = 250 CFH, AL‑425 = 425 CFH, AL‑800 = 800 CFH—verify cut sheets). imacsystems.com, Process Solutions
  8. Regulator selection: Verify capacity at actual inlet/outlet setpoints (e.g., B42 residential; larger B31/B531 or Sensus 243 as loads increase). Use the published capacity tables for your trim. dresserutility.com, istec-corp.com, marshallwnelson.com
  9. Interior piping check: Remind the customer/installer that interior piping remains under NFPA 54 methods (code tables/equations), not this diversity table. Mass.gov
  10. Document HHV, chosen column, seasonal worst case, meter/regulator models, and assumptions.

6) Why ASHRAE supports a conservative column (engineering rationale)

  • Coincident‐peak orientation. ASHRAE’s vocabulary and tools emphasize coincident conditions (e.g., “mean coincident” weather variables) and coincident demand, which explicitly target peak simultaneity—appropriate when you want sizing margin. ASHRAE, terminology.ashrae.org
  • End‑use peaking (DHW). ASHRAE’s Service Water Heating chapter (Applications Handbook) treats probable peak hour and simultaneous draws, a foundation for higher simultaneity in residential DHW compared with the lenient approach. (DOE/PNNL and Federal Register references cite this ASHRAE chapter explicitly.) The Department of Energy's Energy.gov, Federal Register
  • Diversity widely used in ASHRAE design. Even outside gas loads, ASHRAE documents routinely apply diversity factors (e.g., ventilation, occupancy), reinforcing the methodology’s conservatism for peak sizing. ASHRAE

8) When to choose the ASHRAE (Conservative) column

Use this column when any of the following apply:

  • Long services or small diameters where pressure drop limits are tight (e.g., long ¾″ PE runs).
  • High concurrency is likely (multi‑zone heating calling together; large households; holiday cooking during design‑day heating).
  • Uncertain/incomplete appliance inventory or critical loads where undersizing risk must be minimized.
  • Complex end‑uses where probable peak hour and simultaneous draws (e.g., DHW) can be significant, in line with ASHRAE’s service water heating treatment. The Department of Energy's Energy.gov

2) What “IAPMO (Lenient)” means here

  • It’s an operator‑standardized mapping, not a direct copy of a single IAPMO table. IAPMO’s Uniform Mechanical Code (UMC) recognizes diversity/demand factors and, in specific contexts (notably manufactured home communities), provides gas piping demand‑factor tables. That code‑level recognition underpins the notion that simultaneity can be significantly less than 100% for some end uses—hence a “lenient” column for utility service/meter sizing. (epubs.iapmo.org, ANSI Webstore)
  • Where UMC shows this explicitly. UMC editions include Chapter 13 (Fuel Gas Piping) and appendices addressing community gas systems with “Demand Factors for Use in Calculating Gas Piping Systems in Manufactured Home Communities.” (e.g., Table D 107.1 in the 2015/2024 UMC indices). Several jurisdictions echo similar tables. These are not one‑to‑one with your appliance list, but they establish the principle that demand factors are part of IAPMO’s fuel gas toolkit. (epubs.iapmo.org, EncodePlus)
  • IAPMO’s broader diversity work. IAPMO also develops probabilistic diversity methods on the water side (e.g., the Water Demand Calculator), illustrating the organization’s longstanding use of diversity to avoid oversizing peak flows. (This supports—but does not define—our gas diversity mapping.) (IAPMO)

Bottom line: The IAPMO (Lenient) column is a utility practice aligned with IAPMO’s acceptance of demand/diversity concepts—used to right‑size utility‑owned assets—not to replace interior piping code methods.

3) Regulatory boundary in MA, NH, and ME (why this is utility‑side only)

  • Interior fuel gas code in all three states → NFPA 54 (National Fuel Gas Code), as adopted/amended locally:
    • Massachusetts: 248 CMR 5.00 (Amendments to NFPA 54). (Mass.gov)
    • New Hampshire: State‑adopted codes list 2021 NFPA 54 (via Saf‑FMO 300); NH building code notes IFGC fuel‑gas chapters are not adopted—fuel gas is under the NH Fire Code (NFPA 54). (NH DHHS, Merrimack, NH)

5) How to apply (operator procedure)

  1. Inventory each appliance’s nameplate BTU/h.
  2. Pick one column for the job—here, IAPMO (Lenient) for all appliances.
  3. Multiply nameplate × factor to get per‑appliance diversified BTU/h.
  4. Compute Winter Peak (heat + DHW + cooking + dryer + fireplaces + generator) and Summer Peak (pool/spa + DHW + cooking + outdoor + generator).
  5. Design to the larger of Winter vs Summer diversified sums.
  6. Convert to CFH: CFH ≈ BTU/h ÷ HHV. If your system HHV isn’t handy, the U.S. delivered‑gas average is about 1,036 Btu/ft³ (use local value if available). (U.S. Energy Information Administration)
  7. Meter selection: Choose a meter whose rated CFH at ½″ w.c. ΔP ≥ diversified CFH (e.g., AC‑250 = 250 CFH, AL‑425 = 425 CFH, AL‑800 = 800 CFH—verify current cut sheets). (pmt.honeywell.com, imacsystems.com, Process Solutions)
  8. Regulator selection: Verify capacity at actual inlet/outlet setpoints from published tables (e.g., Itron B42 residential; Itron B31/B531 or Sensus 243 at higher loads). (istec-corp.com, Cici Boiler Rooms)
  9. Interior piping reminder: The customer’s interior piping is still sized by NFPA 54 methods (maximum demand basis). (Fireplace and Chimney Professionals)
  10. Document HHV, chosen column, seasonal worst case, meter/regulator models, and assumptions.

6) Why a “lenient” column is technically defensible

  • UMC acknowledges demand/diversity explicitly. UMC includes demand‑factor tables for gas distribution in manufactured home communities and points to diversity in its content—formal recognition that not all connected gas loads peak at once. Using a lower simultaneity mapping for utility service/meter design is consistent with this code philosophy (even though MA/NH/ME interior work uses NFPA 54). (epubs.iapmo.org, ANSI Webstore)
  • IAPMO develops diversity tools. On the plumbing side, IAPMO’s Water Demand Calculator is a probabilistic diversity method that materially reduces peak design flows compared with legacy fixture‑unit tables—an example of right‑sizing via diversity that informs our “lenient” stance for gas utility assets. (IAPMO)
  • Local codes still prevail indoors. NFPA 54 in MA/NH/ME requires interior piping be sized to maximum demand/total connected hourly load, so keep this lenient approach outside the building piping boundary (service, meter, service regulator). (Fireplace and Chimney Professionals)

7) Worked examples (using IAPMO (Lenient) factors)

Assumptions: HHV = 1,036 Btu/ft³; meter ratings at ½″ w.c. ΔP. (U.S. Energy Information Administration, pmt.honeywell.com)

Example A — Typical single‑family home (winter‑peaking)

Appliances: Furnace 100 kBtuh; DHW tank <50 gal 40 kBtuh; Range 65 kBtuh; Dryer 22 kBtuh; Direct‑vent fireplace 30 kBtuh.

Factors: 70%, 30%, 10%, 20%, 15%.

  • Furnace: 100,000 × 0.70 = 70,000 Btu/h
  • DHW: 40,000 × 0.30 = 12,000
  • Range: 65,000 × 0.10 = 6,500
  • Dryer: 22,000 × 0.20 = 4,400
  • Fireplace: 30,000 × 0.15 = 4,500

Winter diversified total = 97,400 Btu/h ⇒ 97,400 / 1,036 = 94.0 CFH.

Implication: AC‑250 (250 CFH) is ample; standard residential regulator (B42) is likely adequate—verify table for actual inlet/outlet setpoints. (pmt.honeywell.com, istec-corp.com)

Example B — Large home with combi boiler + standby generator (winter‑peaking)

Appliances: Combi 199 kBtuh; Range 65 kBtuh; Dryer 22 kBtuh; DV fireplace 30 kBtuh; 22 kW generator (use 327 scfh NG at full load from current spec). (Generac Power Systems)

Factors: 75%, 10%, 20%, 15%, 100% for generator.

  • Combi: 199,000 × 0.75 = 149,250 Btu/h
  • Range: 65,000 × 0.10 = 6,500
  • Dryer: 22,000 × 0.20 = 4,400
  • Fireplace: 30,000 × 0.15 = 4,500
  • Generator: 327 scfh × 1,036 = 338,772 Btu/h

Winter diversified total = 503,422 Btu/h ⇒ 503,422 / 1,036 = 485.9 CFH.

Implication: AL‑425 (425 CFH) is undersized; select AL‑800 (800 CFH) and a higher‑capacity service regulator (e.g., B31/B531 or Sensus 243) set/trimmed per the capacity tables at your inlet/outlet. (imacsystems.com, Process Solutions, istec-corp.com, Cici Boiler Rooms)

Example C — Summer‑peaking home with pool heater

Appliances: Pool heater 400 kBtuh; DHW tank <50 gal 40 kBtuh; Range 65 kBtuh; Outdoor kitchen 90 kBtuh.

Factors: 100%, 30%, 10%, 5%.

  • Pool: 400,000 × 1.00 = 400,000 Btu/h
  • DHW: 40,000 × 0.30 = 12,000
  • Range: 65,000 × 0.10 = 6,500
  • Outdoor kitchen: 90,000 × 0.05 = 4,500

Summer diversified total = 423,000 Btu/h ⇒ 423,000 / 1,036 = 408.3 CFH.

Implication: Borderline for AL‑425; consider AL‑800 for margin and regulator headroom. (imacsystems.com)

8) When to choose the IAPMO (Lenient) column

Use this column when any apply:

  • Loads are strongly non‑coincident (e.g., pool/spa, snow‑melt, large outdoor cooking) versus winter heating.
  • The goal is right‑sizing utility assets in dense or cost‑sensitive areas, with good appliance inventory and confidence in seasonal usage patterns.
  • You have short service runs / adequate inlet pressure and want to avoid unnecessary up‑sizing while maintaining compliance on the utility side.

Do not use these lenient factors to size interior piping governed by NFPA 54 in MA/NH/ME. (Fireplace and Chimney Professionals)

Section 4: Final Notes

Important Disclaimer

This calculator is an estimation tool intended for planning and preliminary assessment. The diversity factors provided are based on common industry standards but may not reflect the specific requirements of your local jurisdiction. The final authority is always the local utility and the applicable building codes (Authority Having Jurisdiction - AHJ). Always verify sizing requirements with a qualified professional or your local gas utility before installation.

Sources & References

  1. ASHRAE Handbook—Fundamentals: Published by the American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers, this multi-volume handbook is widely considered the cornerstone of modern HVAC&R engineering. The Fundamentals volume provides the foundational principles and data for the entire industry. Chapters on load calculations and energy estimating detail the methodologies for applying diversity. ASHRAE's approach is "conservative," meaning its diversity factors are typically higher, resulting in a larger calculated load. This builds in a significant margin of safety, ensuring a system is robust enough to handle near-peak conditions without failure, which is critical for system reliability and longevity. Learn more about the ASHRAE Handbook
  2. Gas Utility Engineering Standards: While not publicly published, the internal engineering and design manuals of local gas utilities are the ultimate authority for service sizing in their territory. These standards are developed from decades of real-world, localized usage data and empirical analysis. They provide the practical basis for sizing meters, regulators, and service lines. The mandate for using a 100% diversity factor for life-safety equipment (generators) and mission-critical systems (snow-melt boilers, pool heaters) comes directly from these standards. The utility's primary goal is to guarantee the absolute reliability and safety of the gas grid, which means ensuring these critical appliances have an uninterruptible fuel supply at their maximum required rating, regardless of any other loads in the building.
  3. IAPMO Uniform Mechanical Code (UMC): The International Association of Plumbing and Mechanical Officials develops model codes that are adopted into law by local and state governments. The UMC provides the minimum standards for the safe installation of mechanical systems, including gas piping downstream of the utility meter. Its gas pipe sizing tables (often based on the "longest pipe run" method) inherently account for a degree of diversity. The factors associated with IAPMO are sometimes considered more "lenient" because they are designed to meet the minimum safety code for a standard installation, whereas an engineering standard like ASHRAE is focused on designing a system for optimal performance and a higher degree of robustness.
